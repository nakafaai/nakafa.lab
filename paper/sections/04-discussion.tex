\section{Discussion}

The current evidence map supports one disciplined conclusion: the affordable
cure-oriented search should benchmark itself against stem-cell and fetal
hemoglobin biology, but should not claim a cure without assay and clinical
evidence. The most credible discovery lane is an HPFH-like program that combines
HbF/F-cell induction with globin-chain balance, erythroid maturation, mature
red-cell hemolysis exclusion, and iron-risk interpretation.

This also changes the immediate operational priority. Before adding more
candidate materials, the project should complete the de-identified case packet,
ask a hematologist whether the packet is screenable for existing therapies or
referrals, and prepare a small qualified-lab work order for dual HbF and
alpha-globin-burden readouts.

\section{Limitations}

This manuscript has several important limitations:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item No wet-lab experiment has been performed by Nakafa Lab.
  \item No patient sample, animal study, clinical trial, or treatment
  intervention has been performed by Nakafa Lab.
  \item ClinicalTrials.gov records are registry metadata and require expert
  curation before mechanism or access claims are made.
  \item Natural-product and Islamic-source lanes generate hypotheses and
  ethical framing, not biomedical proof.
  \item The current case context is incomplete and de-identified; it cannot be
  used to infer patient eligibility.
\end{itemize}
