# Thalassemia Program

## Starting Frame

Thalassemia is an inherited hemoglobin disorder. Severe beta-thalassemia can
require lifelong red blood cell transfusion and iron chelation. Curative or
disease-modifying options exist, but access and cost remain major barriers.

The research goal is not to ignore current medicine. The goal is to understand
it deeply, then search for credible, affordable paths that could help patients
in Indonesia and beyond.

## Known Treatment Landscape

- Standard care: transfusion plus iron chelation.
- Potential cure for selected patients: hematopoietic stem-cell transplant.
- Disease-modifying therapy: luspatercept for transfusion burden reduction.
- Gene and cell therapy: betibeglogene autotemcel and exagamglogene autotemcel.
- Oral therapy: mitapivat was approved by the FDA in December 2025 for anemia in
  adults with alpha- or beta-thalassemia.
- Disease-modifying iron-axis comparators: hepcidin, ferroportin, `TMPRSS6`,
  erythroferrone, vamifeport, minihepcidin, and iron-restriction biology.

## First Questions

- Which mechanisms have the best evidence for reducing transfusion burden?
- Which approved therapies are inaccessible mainly because of price or
  infrastructure?
- Which lower-cost compounds have credible fetal hemoglobin or erythropoiesis
  evidence?
- Which interventions can reduce iron toxicity or ineffective erythropoiesis
  without worsening anemia?
- What Indonesia-specific constraints change the research priority?
- Can any bee-product hypothesis be made testable without unsafe claims?

## Near-Term Outputs

- Landscape review of approved and investigational therapies.
- Indonesia access and affordability memo.
- Mechanism map for fetal hemoglobin, erythropoiesis, and iron overload.
- Hypothesis cards ranked by evidence, safety, and affordability.
