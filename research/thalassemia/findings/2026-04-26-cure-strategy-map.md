# Finding: Cure Strategy Map

Date checked: 2026-04-26
Evidence label: Cure-path synthesis

## Working Conclusion

For severe transfusion-dependent thalassemia, the most credible definition of
`cure` is durable transfusion independence with safe hemoglobin, controlled iron
burden, and long-term survival without unacceptable toxicity.

The current cure-class evidence points to hematopoietic stem cells: replace
them, correct them, or reprogram their hemoglobin output.

## Cure-Class Paths

| Path | Mechanism | Why it can cure | Main blocker |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Allogeneic HSCT | Replace blood-forming stem cells from donor | New marrow can produce healthier red cells | Donor match, GVHD, conditioning toxicity, cost |
| Lentiviral gene addition | Add functional beta-globin program to autologous HSCs | Patient's own corrected cells can produce useful hemoglobin | Manufacturing, conditioning, price, long-term follow-up |
| CRISPR HbF reactivation | Edit autologous HSCs to reactivate fetal hemoglobin | HbF compensates for defective beta-globin | Conditioning, access, cost, off-target and durability monitoring |
| Future in vivo editing | Deliver edit directly in the body | Could remove ex vivo manufacturing bottleneck | Not yet established for thalassemia care |
| Future small molecule cure-like path | Mimic HbF/gene-therapy effect without cell therapy | Could be cheaper and scalable | No proven cure-level molecule yet |

## Key Evidence

- Exagamglogene autotemcel reactivates HbF by CRISPR editing of the erythroid
  `BCL11A` enhancer in autologous CD34+ cells. In a 2024 NEJM phase 3 interim
  analysis, 32 of 35 evaluable TDT patients achieved transfusion independence.
- Betibeglogene autotemcel shows that lentiviral beta-globin gene addition can
  produce transfusion independence in many TDT patients, but cost, conditioning,
  and long-term safety monitoring remain major issues.
- TIF 2025 still treats HSCT as a curative option for selected patients, with
  risk strongly shaped by donor, age, organ damage, and transplant expertise.

## Nakafa Lab Research Direction

The next cure-oriented question should be:

Can we identify a low-cost, scalable path that approximates the effect of gene
therapy by improving fetal hemoglobin, globin-chain balance, erythroid
maturation, or red-cell survival without full transplant infrastructure?

This does not mean abandoning gene therapy research. It means using gene therapy
as the biological proof-of-concept and searching for cheaper ways to reach the
same endpoint.

## Immediate Research Tasks

1. Build a target map around `BCL11A`, `HBG1`, `HBG2`, `HBB`, `HBA1/HBA2`,
   `KLF1`, `HBS1L-MYB`, `PKLR`, and erythroid maturation pathways.
2. Separate cure-class endpoints from symptom endpoints.
3. Search existing compounds and natural products only if they connect to those
   targets or endpoints.
4. Track active trials in gene therapy, gene editing, HbF induction, and
   erythroid maturation.
5. Map which parts of the cure pipeline are expensive: cell collection,
   conditioning, viral/vector manufacturing, editing, hospitalization, and
   long-term follow-up.
