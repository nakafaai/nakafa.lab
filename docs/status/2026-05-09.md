## Strongest Research Lanes

1. Public responder qualification: convert replies into role, scope, evidence
   cue, and safe state.
2. Minimum hematologist packet: keep diagnosis, genetics, transfusion burden,
   immune/blood-bank, iron, organ, and referral questions in one reviewable
   checklist.
3. Advanced-therapy access mapping: use public registry records to understand
   screening pathways and centers without implying eligibility.
4. Transfusion and immune record completion: quantify burden and collect
   blood-bank context before interpretation.
5. Iron and organ-risk monitoring: separate ferritin trend, MRI iron measures,
   chelation safety records, and organ monitoring instead of using one marker as
   a shortcut.
6. Assay feasibility and privacy tooling: turn non-clinical help into scoped
   tasks that do not require private records.

## Patient-Context Data Still Needed

- Molecular diagnosis records: `HBB`, `HBA1/HBA2`, HPFH or delta-beta context,
  and hemoglobin-analysis timing relative to transfusion.
- Transfusion burden records: dates, pre/post hemoglobin, unit volume, red-cell
  fraction when available, body weight, reactions, and interval pattern.
- Immune and blood-bank records: ABO/Rh, extended phenotype or genotype, Rh/Kell
  matching, antibody screen, alloantibodies, DAT/direct Coombs, and reaction
  history.
- Iron and organ records: ferritin trend, liver iron concentration, cardiac
  `T2*`, endocrine monitoring, renal/liver safety labs, and chelator identity
  with monitoring context.
- Advanced-therapy screening context: prior transplant or gene-therapy
  discussions, donor/HLA status if available, organ-function constraints, center
  location constraints, and public registry IDs mentioned by qualified contacts.
