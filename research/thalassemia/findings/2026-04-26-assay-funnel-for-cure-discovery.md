# Finding: Assay Funnel for Cure Discovery

Date checked: 2026-04-26
Evidence label: discovery workflow

## Working Conclusion

Nakafa Lab should not move any candidate toward patients until it passes a
basic assay funnel. For cure-oriented discovery, the funnel starts with HbF and
erythroid biology, then eliminates candidates that damage red cells or create
unacceptable toxicity risk.

## Assay Funnel

| Stage | Question | Example endpoint |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1. Reporter screen | Does the candidate increase `HBG1/HBG2` or HbF signal? | HUDEP2 `HBG1` reporter, gamma-globin mRNA |
| 2. Erythroid differentiation | Does the signal survive in erythroid cells? | CD34+ erythroid differentiation, HbF, F-cells |
| 3. Thalassemia model | Does it help a beta-thalassemia-like system? | beta-globin knockdown or patient-derived erythroid cells |
| 4. Red-cell safety | Does it damage mature red cells? | hemolysis, membrane fragility, oxidative hemolysis |
| 5. General toxicity | Does it harm liver/kidney/immune-relevant cells? | cytotoxicity and stress-response assays |
| 6. Translation review | Is there a plausible human path? | dose, route, monitoring, affordability, clinician review |

## Why This Matters

Bee-derived materials, venom peptides, and natural extracts can look active in
generic antioxidant screens but fail the thalassemia endpoint. Worse, some may
damage red-cell membranes or trigger allergy. A candidate is useful only if it
improves thalassemia-relevant biology without creating a bigger safety problem.

## Minimum Promotion Rule

A candidate can move from exploratory to active hypothesis only if it shows:

- HbF, globin balance, erythroid maturation, or transfusion-burden relevance;
- no unacceptable hemolysis signal;
- known product identity and batch standardization;
- plausible dosing and monitoring path;
- affordability path for Indonesia;
- clinician and toxicologist review plan.
