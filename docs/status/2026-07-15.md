# Daily Research Journal: 2026-07-15

Audience: founder / researcher
Status: sapablursen monotherapy deprioritized, not medical advice

## Question And Decision

**Question:** Does the published phase 2a sapablursen result justify retaining
`TMPRSS6` antisense monotherapy as an affordable route toward durable
transfusion independence?

**Decision:** `deprioritize_sapablursen_monotherapy`. Keep the broader
hepcidin-ferroportin axis as `comparator_only`.

## What Changed

- **Fact:** The published study enrolled 29 adults with genotypically confirmed
  non-transfusion-dependent beta-thalassemia into three open-label active-dose
  cohorts. It had no placebo cohort.
- **Fact:** Sapablursen did not meet the primary hemoglobin endpoint at week 27
  or the secondary hemoglobin and liver-iron endpoints at week 53. Serum
  hepcidin did not increase consistently, and the registry says the study was
  terminated because efficacy missed the sponsor's target profile.
- **Safety boundary:** The paper reports generally favorable tolerability and
  the registry reports no deaths. Three participants had serious adverse events
  across cohorts, but the registry display does not establish causation.
- **Contradiction:** Earlier mouse and non-thalassemia studies supported
  hepcidin induction. The pharmacodynamic effect did not translate consistently
  in this `NTDT` cohort.
- **Hypothesis:** Companion mouse work makes high erythroferrone (`ERFE`)
  and severe iron loading plausible resistance mechanisms. This is not proof
  that either marker predicts human response or that every `TMPRSS6` modality
  will fail.
- **Interpretation:** The negative human endpoints are enough to deprioritize
  sapablursen monotherapy, but not the entire iron-axis target class.
- **Confidence:** High for the negative sapablursen endpoints; moderate for the
  proposed resistance mechanism; low for extrapolation to other modalities,
  transfusion-dependent disease, or cure.
- **Affordability and access:** Repeated injectable antisense therapy plus
  hepcidin, anemia, iron, and MRI monitoring is not a low-infrastructure route.
  Without demonstrated benefit, sapablursen has no evidence-backed
  affordability case for thalassemia.

## Falsification And Next Action

Restore a `TMPRSS6` modality only when a human thalassemia study shows target
engagement, prespecified hepcidin or iron improvement, and hemoglobin or
transfusion benefit, with resistance evaluated against baseline `ERFE` and iron
burden. Cure-lane promotion would additionally require durable transfusion
independence and a documented total delivered-cost path.

Next decisive action: do not allocate Nakafa assay or outreach effort to
sapablursen-specific work. Reassess the broader lane only when another modality
reports both pharmacodynamic and clinical human thalassemia outcomes.

**Open question:** Can another modality overcome high-`ERFE` or high-iron
resistance and produce both pharmacodynamic and clinical benefit in human
thalassemia?
