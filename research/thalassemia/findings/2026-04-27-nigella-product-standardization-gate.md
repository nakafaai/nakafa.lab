# Finding: Nigella Product Standardization Gate

Date checked: 2026-04-27
Evidence label: quality-control gate for prophetic-medicine adjunct research

## Working Conclusion

`Nigella sativa` cannot be treated as one reproducible intervention unless the
material is chemically standardized. Two product-quality studies show large
variation in thymoquinone (`TQ`) content across commercial black-seed oils and
capsules. This means a thalassemia trial signal cannot be copied to a random
retail oil, capsule, powder, or seed batch.

For Nakafa Lab, any black-seed lane must specify:

- material type: seed, fixed oil, essential oil, extract, capsule, or isolated
  TQ;
- batch identity and manufacturer;
- HPLC or equivalent TQ quantification;
- storage, light, heat, oxidation, and expiry handling;
- contaminant testing and clinician safety review.

## Evidence Snapshot

| Source | Finding | Research implication |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Khaikin et al. 2022, Nutrients | six oils and five oil-containing capsules had TQ content from `3.08` to `809.4 mg/100 g` | commercial products are not equivalent |
| Khaikin et al. 2022 | capsules varied by about `31x` when the expired sample was excluded; bottled oils varied by almost `18x` | formulation and storage matter |
| Khaikin et al. 2022 | authors argued TQ content should be declared on black-seed oil products | TQ assay should be mandatory before trials |
| Alkhatib et al. 2020, Journal of Pharmacy and Bioallied Sciences | Malaysian commercial products ranged from `0.07%` to `1.88%` TQ by weight | retail product labels are not enough |
| Alkhatib et al. 2020 | highest product was about `27x` higher than the lowest | dose translation from one product to another is invalid |

## Why This Matters for Thalassemia

The 2024 pediatric beta-thalassemia major trial measured and discussed TQ
content in the study material. That detail is not cosmetic. If TQ is part of
the hypothesized iron-chelation or antioxidant mechanism, then unknown TQ
content makes the intervention uninterpretable.

This is especially important in transfusion-dependent thalassemia, where
patients may already have iron overload, chelation therapy, liver monitoring,
immune complications, and frequent transfusions. A poorly characterized
supplement could create false hope, inconsistent exposure, or interaction
risks.

## Practical Research Gate

Before `Nigella sativa` is allowed into any Nakafa Lab candidate comparison, it
must pass this checklist:

- PubMed/registry source exists for the specific question being tested;
- exact material and batch are recorded;
- TQ content is measured, not inferred from marketing;
- antioxidant/iron endpoints are separated from HbF and transfusion endpoints;
- hemolysis and liver/kidney safety gates are defined;
- the protocol is reviewed by a licensed clinician before patient-facing use.

## Research Decision

Keep `Nigella sativa` as a medium-priority adjunct lane only when the material
is standardized. Without standardization, mark the candidate as unusable for
serious thalassemia research, even if it has Islamic or traditional-medicine
motivation.

This makes the black-seed lane stricter, not weaker: the prophetic-medicine
anchor is preserved, but the research standard becomes high enough for doctors
to evaluate.
