# Finding: Disease Taxonomy Primer

Date checked: 2026-04-26
Evidence label: Clinical genetics synthesis

## Working Conclusion

`Thalassemia` is not one disease state. Research must separate genotype,
phenotype, and transfusion need before discussing treatment.

## Core Distinctions

- Alpha-thalassemia usually involves reduced alpha-globin production, often from
  `HBA1` and `HBA2` gene deletions.
- Beta-thalassemia involves reduced or absent beta-globin production from `HBB`
  variants.
- HbE/beta-thalassemia combines a hemoglobin E allele with a beta-thalassemia
  allele and is clinically common in Southeast Asia.
- Carrier state is not the same as severe disease. Carriers may have mild blood
  count changes but usually do not require lifelong transfusion.
- TDT means regular transfusion is required for survival or disease control.
- NTDT means the patient does not require regular transfusion for survival, but
  may still develop serious complications.

## Why It Matters

Therapy reasoning changes by subtype:

- HbF induction is most directly relevant to beta-globin disorders.
- Alpha/beta globin balance matters for disease severity.
- HbE/beta-thalassemia may not behave like classic beta-thalassemia major.
- Alpha-thalassemia and HbH disease need different diagnostic and treatment
  reasoning.
- Gene therapy and gene-editing indications are usually specific to
  transfusion-dependent beta-thalassemia.

## Research Implication

The first fork in any case review is:

1. Confirm subtype and genotype.
2. Classify as TDT or NTDT by actual transfusion pattern and clinical need.
3. Identify complications: iron overload, spleen status, alloimmunization,
   autoimmune disease, endocrine issues, liver disease, heart disease, and bone
   disease.
4. Only then compare therapy options.
